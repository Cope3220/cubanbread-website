02Must-fix before launch

Eight items.
None take more than an hour.

These are the gaps that will actually cost rankings or AI mentions on day one. Ranked by impact, not effort.

01

Homepage <title> doesn't say "Tampa"

Critical file: index.html · line 6

The single highest-leverage local-SEO lever for a Tampa-Ybor bakery is "Tampa" in the homepage title. Right now the homepage targets "Wholesale Cuban Bread Since 1915" but not "Cuban Bread Tampa" — a phrase searched roughly 10–15× more often. Title is also 70+ chars; aim for ≤60.

Current
<title>La Segunda Central Bakery — Wholesale Cuban Bread Since 1915</title>
Recommended
<title>La Segunda · Authentic Cuban Bread in Tampa Since 1915</title>
02

Homepage meta description is too long

Critical file: index.html · line 7

Current description is 530+ characters. Google truncates at ~155–160. Everything after "active in 18 states today" is invisible in SERPs. Rewrite as a tight, lead-with-place-and-claim sentence that AI Overviews can lift wholesale.

Recommended (153 chars)
<meta name="description" content="Authentic Cuban bread baked in Ybor City,
Tampa since 1915. Family-owned, 4th generation. Wholesale & foodservice
in 35+ states — direct line to Giuseppe."/>

05

No Organization or WebSite schema

High file: index.html <head>

You have great Bakery schema, but Google's Knowledge Panel and the sitelinks search box are driven by Organization + WebSite. Add both to the homepage only. The Organization @id should link back to the Bakery @id so they reconcile as the same entity.

Add to index.html <head>
<script type="application/ld+json">
{

  "@graph":[

      "@type":"Organization",
      "@id":"https://www.cubanbread.com/#org",
      "name":"La Segunda Central Bakery",
      "alternateName":["La Segunda","La Segunda Wholesale"],
      "url":"https://www.cubanbread.com/",
      "logo":"https://www.cubanbread.com/assets/logo-word.png",
      "foundingDate":"1915",
      "founder":{"@type":"Person","name":"Juan Moré"},
      "sameAs":[
        "https://www.instagram.com/lasegundabakery/",
        "https://www.facebook.com/LaSegundaBakery/",
        "https://x.com/LaSegundaBakery"
      ],
      "mainEntityOfPage":{"@id":"https://www.cubanbread.com/#bakery"}
    },
    {
      "@type":"WebSite",
      "@id":"https://www.cubanbread.com/#website",
      "url":"https://www.cubanbread.com/",

12

Fix hreflang with proper <head> declarations

Medium file: contact.html, distribution.html, /es/*

Inline <a hreflang="es"> in the footer is a user-facing nicety. Google looks for paired <link rel="alternate"> tags in <head>. Without them, the ES variants don't get credited as alternates and you risk duplicate-content scoring against the EN page.

In contact.html <head>
<link rel="alternate" hreflang="en-US" href="https://www.cubanbread.com/contact.html"/>
<link rel="alternate" hreflang="es-US" href="https://www.cubanbread.com/es/contact.html"/>
<link rel="alternate" hreflang="x-default" href="https://www.cubanbread.com/contact.html"/>
In es/contact.html <head> (mirror)
<link rel="alternate" hreflang="en-US" href="https://www.cubanbread.com/contact.html"/>
<link rel="alternate" hreflang="es-US" href="https://www.cubanbread.com/es/contact.html"/>
<link rel="alternate" hreflang="x-default" href="https://www.cubanbread.com/contact.html"/>

Repeat for distribution.html ↔ es/distribution.html. Also add both ES URLs to sitemap.xml.

Who invented Cuban bread?

Cuban bread as Americans know it was developed in Tampa, Florida — specifically in the immigrant neighborhood of Ybor City — in the early 1900s. La Segunda Central Bakery, founded in 1915 by Juan Moré, is the oldest continuously operating Cuban bakery in the United States and is credited with codifying the long, palmetto-leaf-scored loaf that defines the Tampa-style Cuban sandwich.

Where is the original Cuban bread bakery?

La Segunda Central Bakery is at 2512 N. 15th Street, Tampa, FL 33605, in the heart of historic Ybor City. The bakery has operated continuously from the same neighborhood since 1915 and remains family-owned and -operated by the fourth generation of the Moré family.

Is Cuban bread actually from Cuba?

The recipe has Spanish-Cuban roots, but the loaf itself — the long, soft-interior, palmetto-leaf-split version sold in American sandwich shops — was perfected in Tampa, not Havana. Catalan immigrant Juan Moré brought a Spanish-Cuban formula to Ybor City in 1915 and built the loaf that has defined the regional bread for more than a century.

What makes La Segunda Cuban bread different?

Three things: high-gluten spring wheat flour milled to our specification, a slow overnight fermentation, and the palmetto leaf laid down the center of every loaf before baking — the score Juan Moré used on opening day in 1915. The recipe and method have not changed in four generations.

For pricing, contact Giuseppe at giuseppe@cubanbread.com or (813) 810-6920; replies within one business day.

La Segunda product is reachable in more than 35 states through 46 distributor locations across the United States and is actively moving in 18 states today. Frozen product has a 270-day shelf life at 0°F. Tampa Bay buyers also receive fresh-daily delivery on La Segunda's own trucks.

What is the minimum order for wholesale?

For local Tampa Bay direct delivery: 10 loaves per drop, no contract. For frozen distribution outside Tampa Bay, minimums are set by the broadline distributor (Sysco, US Foods, PFG, GFS, Cheney Brothers, Ben E. Keith) and scale by case rather than loaf. Single-location operators and multi-unit groups both welcome.

Is the bakery open to the public?

Yes. The retail bakery at 2512 N. 15th Street, Tampa has served Ybor City since 1915 and remains open daily for fresh Cuban bread, hoagie rolls, bucket breads, basket bread, and pastries.

05Beautiful, but…

Aesthetic choices with a discoverability tax.

None of these are wrong. They're the cost of a strong art direction. Knowing they cost something lets you decide which to keep on purpose.

— Roman numerals

"Est. MCMXV" & "© MCMXV – MMXXVI"

Beautiful. Unreadable to LLMs. The schema carries "1915" so search engines know, but on-page text scrapers and AI quote-extractors won't equate MCMXV with 1915. Keep them, but always pair with the Arabic year in the same paragraph — which the hero already does.

— Decorative italics

Red italic emphasis on nouns

The site uses <em> as a visual flourish, not for semantic emphasis. Most of the time this is fine, but in body copy it occasionally italicizes brand-critical words (Original, recipe, Ybor City) that you'd want to stand out for AI. Consider <span class="em"> for the cosmetic case and reserve <em> for sentences where you genuinely want the engine to weight the word.

— Split headlines

Hero H1 across four <span> lines

"The / Original / Cuban / Bread." — typographically gorgeous, semantically fine (it parses as one string). Just confirm in any future revisions that you don't accidentally introduce a <br> or display:block tweak that interrupts the parsed text node.

— Product placeholders

CSS-gradient "product" cards

The radial-gradient placeholders are tasteful as a launch holding pattern but they're invisible to Google Images, Pinterest, and AI shopping carousels. Every product card is an opportunity to win a thumbnail in a result. This is the single highest-ROI visual upgrade you can ship.

— Map graphic

Animated SVG / Leaflet distribution map

Stunning. Leaflet renders into a canvas though, so the state names are not crawlable text. You already include them in the body and in areaServed schema — that's the right move. Just be sure that if you ever remove the text fallback, you don't simultaneously lose crawler access to the state list.

— Hero headline narrowness

"The Original Cuban Bread." is missing two words

The H1 doesn't mention "La Segunda" or "Tampa". This is the single most important text on the site for both Google and AI. Either expand the visible headline (which costs the typography), or add a screen-reader-only span (see Recommendation 14) — but don't ship the page without one of those two.

— Italic Saveur, NYT, etc.

"As Featured In" links use only publication names

Link text is just Saveur, Bon Appétit, etc. Search engines weight anchor text. Consider adding hover-revealed or visually-secondary descriptors ("La Segunda in Saveur") on the <a>'s aria-label or title attribute — invisible to design, valuable to crawlers.

— No real photos yet

og:image is the same file on every page

Every page's Open Graph image is /assets/og-image.jpg. Social shares of Products, Distribution, and Our Story will all preview as the same loaf. Once you have product photography, swap a page-specific OG image for each.

06AI search readiness

How ChatGPT, Perplexity, and Google AI Overviews will actually read this site.

Three principles drive AI-engine citations: clear factual statements, verifiable third-party signal, and a Q&A surface that maps to user intent. You're 7/10 on the first two and 3/10 on the third.

A

Make the "oldest / original" claim explicit and dated

High

Right now you say "the first name in authentic Cuban bread" and "America's original Cuban bread bakery" — but those are marketing claims. AI engines love claims that come with verifiable specifics. State plainly, once, on the homepage and once on /our-story: "Founded 1915. The oldest continuously operating Cuban bakery in the United States." Then back it with a press citation. This single sentence is what gets quoted when someone asks ChatGPT "who is the original Cuban bread bakery?"

B

Inline-cite your press

Medium

The "As Featured In" row is great brand armor. But for AI engines, a citation is more useful when it lives inline with a claim: "…the most legendary Cuban sandwich in Tampa, according to Southern Living (2025)." One sentence per page that quotes a publication, with the outlet name in body text, is worth more than the entire logo row for AI Overview inclusion.

C

Write for the "second sentence"

Medium

AI Overviews almost always quote the second or third sentence of a paragraph, not the first (the first is treated as a topic sentence). Audit your homepage and Our Story paragraphs so the second sentence of each one is a complete, quotable, attribution-friendly fact about La Segunda. Right now several of those second sentences are decorative ("It comes from a family…") — they read beautifully but don't carry a fact.

D

Use plain-language headings alongside the typographic ones

Low

"From Ybor City to everywhere." is gorgeous and means nothing to a crawler. Either keep it and add an H3 directly underneath ("Distribution network — 35+ states through 46 broadline locations"), or accept that this section will rank for nothing on its own. The section's body copy is strong enough to carry it, but the heading isn't doing work.
